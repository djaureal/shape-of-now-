I
01. The claim · philosophy

No one has ever lived in the present.

The present has no width. Name now and it has already dissolved, like a wave you cannot hold. What stays is the story the mind tells: past and future, stitched into a place that feels like here.

II
02. The evidence · neuroscience

Your now is about eighty milliseconds late.

Light, sound and touch arrive at different speeds. The brain waits, edits, and hands you a moment that has already passed. You have never seen the world — only the mind’s picture of it, rendered late and backdated as truth.

III
03. The stakes · social

If now is built.
someone is building yours.

A present that can be built can be captured; a mind that clings can be led. Attention is the ground of freedom. To stand fully in the moment is the last act of authorship we have left.

IV
04. The answer · the work

So we built a show
to make you feel it.

Not a lecture. A rite of passage in sound and light. @Ephemera — no past, no future — walks ahead like a lamp on the path, and the score carries you, because rhythm is the oldest way the body knows time. For one hour, past and future are exposed as the illusions they are.

the construct of time · philosophy

The only reality that has
ever existed is now.

The past is memory, held now. The future is expectation, modelled now. Neither can be touched — clouds crossing an open sky. The mind presses them together into the thin, living sheet we call the present: not a line we travel, but a shape continuously assembled.

This is old wisdom and new science in the same breath. And it is freedom: if the present is composed, it can be composed differently. To shape light, sound and image into one shared moment is to compose reality itself.

the argument  Presentism holds that only the now exists; eternalism, that all moments exist at once. This work lives in the tension between them. Staging a present that is the only thing real, and revealing it to be authored by a past and a future that are not.

the show · cinematic storytelling

A stage that dissolves the illusion.

Past and future govern how we plan, grieve, hope and fear. Yet neither has ever been touched. On stage, the illusion is taken apart by the three forces that hold it together — until only presence remains.

Image

Show the invisible architecture.

Full-stage projection wraps the audience in the argument. @Ephemera appears as a living diagram of pure presence — form made of light, empty of history.

The effect: you watch time being built and dismantled, at a scale that overwhelms the eye.

Score

The music is the narrator.

The score moves from stillness through cinematic breaks to a single devastating drop — form arising, peaking, dissolving. Sub-bass is felt in the body before it reaches the ears.

The effect: the argument lands in the chest before the mind can intellectualise it.

Stage

There is no fourth wall.

No fourth wall. Dark, vast, architectural — performer, projection and sound as one organism. You are not watching the river. You are in it.

The effect: a room full of strangers awakening to the same moment at the same time.

At the climax, everything converges. Image, score and stage collapse into one moment, and the audience feels the illusion break: the past dissolves, the future vanishes, and what remains is the only thing that was ever real.

show format · tap to open
01

The premise: what it is

+

The Shape of Now is a cinematic audiovisual stage show. A live performance built from original music, full-stage projection, and one luminous figure, @Ephemera. The show tells a single story: that the past and the future are illusions constructed by the brain, and that this illusion fundamentally shapes the way we live, love, grieve and fear.

Three acts build the argument from silence to climax. The audience does not need to understand neuroscience or philosophy. They feel the argument in the music, see it in the visuals, and recognise it in themselves.

02

How it works

+

The stage carries full-width projection behind and above the performer, creating an immersive visual field that extends beyond the proscenium. A multichannel sound system delivers the score with cinematic weight; sub-bass frequencies are felt physically in the chest and floor before they are consciously heard.

Each act escalates the argument. Act I dissolves the audience into a world before time existed. Act II reveals identity as something spread across an illusory past. Act III brings the climax: the moment the illusion collapses and the only real thing, the present, is all that remains. @Ephemera appears throughout as the figure who has never had a past or a future, the witness who proves the argument by existing without one.

03

What it sets out to achieve

+

The show has one aim: to make a room full of people feel, viscerally, that the past and the future are not real, and that this revelation is not terrifying but liberating.

Philosophically, it stages the argument that the present is the only reality, and that past and future are constructions that govern how we live without our consent. Experientially, it sets out to take the audience on a cinematic journey from stillness to catharsis, using music, image and storytelling to make an abstract idea land in the body. Where most media fracture attention and sell the pieces, this show gathers it into one shared, undeniable moment.

04

The audience experience

+

The lights go down. The stage opens onto darkness and a low sub-bass tone that the audience feels before they hear it. @Ephemera appears, luminous and still, in a world that has no past. The opening act is ambient, vast, and slow. Time loosens. The audience settles into a present that feels suspended.

The second act introduces tension. The score builds, the visuals fracture, and the argument emerges: identity is not a point but a wave, spread across an illusory timeline. By the third act the music drives toward a single devastating climax. Image, sound and light collapse together. The past dissolves. The future vanishes. What remains is the only thing that was ever real. Then the stage releases, and the audience breathes.

05

Live performance and touring

+

The show is designed to tour. The core technical rider is a wide-format projection surface, a multichannel PA with sub-bass capability, and stage lighting. The production scales from intimate black-box theatres (300 capacity) to large-format festival stages, with the visual field expanding to fill whatever space is available.

The performer is present on stage throughout as the author and guide, operating within the projected world alongside @Ephemera. The show runs 60–90 minutes without interval. Every performance is the same argument. Every audience receives it differently.

06

The creative team

+

Conceived, written and scored by Tony Funiciello. The original score spans ambient drone, cinematic breaks and dark electronic, drawing on influences from Brian Eno, Massive Attack, Autechre and Depeche Mode. All music is written and produced by hand.

@Ephemera is designed as a character that exists outside time: faceless, translucent, genderless. A figure built from light rather than flesh, with no biography, no memory, no ambition. The purest possible witness. The visual world is brutalist, monochrome, architectural: raw concrete, void backgrounds, cold silver light. No warm tones. No natural landscapes. A world that could only exist in the present, because it has no history to point to.

the narrative · the story the show tells

A story told in
three acts.

No dialogue. One revelation: the past you carry and the future you fear are constructions. @Ephemera — empty of history, empty of expectation — walks with you as the illusion is stripped away.

I
We Measure · before division

It begins before time.

No beat, no division. Only field. @Ephemera rests complete and still, before the mind cuts the world into before and after. This is the ocean, before it is told it is waves.

II
You’re the Wave · the spreading

Then the illusion begins.

The mind divides. It invents a past, projects a future — and a self appears, stretched across a timeline that does not exist. But you were never the particle pinned to a point. You are the wave — and the water was never divided.

III
Beyond the End · the reading

And the illusion breaks.

Everything collapses into one undeniable instant. The body knows before the mind catches up: this is the only moment that has ever existed. Nothing was lost — it was never yours to hold. That is the liberation.

There is no final scene. Because the present never ends. It is constructed, held, broken and rebuilt, endlessly. That is not the tragedy. That is the freedom. The only story there has ever been is happening now.
